Solution

  • Fit the models
## Binomial model
## Might as well write a function!
mlebinom = function(x)
{
  n = length(x) - 1
  v = 0:n
  p = sum(v*x)/sum(n*x)
  logl = sum( x * dbinom(v, n, p, log=TRUE) )
  dat = tibble(
    phat = p,
    logl = logl
  )
  return ( dat )
}

g5_binom = mlebinom(g5$freq)

Solution

Using results from the previous problem, test the null hypothesis of the binomial model versus the alternative hypothesis of the beta-binomial model. Report a test statistic, the sampling distribution of the test statistic assuming the null hypothesis is true, and a numerical estimate of the p-value.

## test statistic
lrt = 2 * (g5_bb$logl - g5_binom$logl)
lrt
## [1] 48.40527
## p-value
1 - pchisq(lrt,1)
## [1] 3.466338e-12
  • The test statistic is 48.4.
  • Under the null hypothesis, the test statistic has a chi-square(1) distribution.
  • The p-value is less than \(4\times 10^{-12}\).

Interpret the results of this hypothesis text in context.

There is strong evidence of heterogeneity in the probability of the sexes of children in different families in Saxony during the time period relative to a model with assumes these probabilities are all equal (\(p < 4\times 10^{-12}\), likelihood ratio test, LRT = 48.4, df = 1).

The statement is not fully justified from the data. There are many values to compare, potentially.

  • Among families with only one child, the proportion that have another child is:
    • 71.6% if they already have a boy;
    • 69.5% if they already have a girl.
    • The difference is about 2% higher for those families with a single boy.
  • Among families with two children, the proportion that have another child is:
    • 56.9% if they already have two boys;
    • 55.4% if they already have one boy and one girl;
    • 57.1% if they already have two girls.
    • Families with one child of each sex are about 1.5% less likely to have another child than if both children are the sme sex.
    • The difference between those with two boys or two girls is small, only 0.2%.
  • Among families with three children, the proportion that have another child is:
    • 52.4% if they already have three boys;
    • 50.6% if they already have two boys and one girl;
    • 50.3% if they already have two girls and one boy;
    • 52.3% if they already have three girls.
  • We could continue to make comparisons for larger families, but the total number of such families is much smaller so that observed differenes are more likely to be affected by small samples.
  • We are missing data from families with all or almost all children of the same sex for families with 7 or more children.

There is a slight trend that families with only one boy are slightly more likely to have another child than those with only one girl, but the differences are very small for families with two and three children.

The larger trend, perhaps, is that families with all children of the same sex are more likely to have an additional child than families with a mix of boys and girls.
